## Sean Ellis 40% Test

**The question:** "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"
- Very disappointed
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed

**Benchmark:** If ≥40% answer "very disappointed" → you likely have PMF.

**Operational advice:**
- Survey quarterly, not once — markets shift
- Segment results — total averages lie
- Only survey users who experienced core value (used product ≥2x in last 2 weeks)
- Superhuman hit 58% by rebuilding for power users and ignoring 80% of responses

## Superhuman's PMF Engine (Rahul Vohra)

### The 4 Survey Questions
1. "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" (Very/Somewhat/Not disappointed)
2. "What type of people do you think would most benefit from [product]?"
3. "What is the main benefit you receive from [product]?"
4. "How can we improve [product] for you?"

### 5-Step Engine

**Step 1 — Segment to find supporters**
Filter responses to find which personas appear in "very disappointed" cohort. Create a High-Expectation Customer (HXC) profile using their own language.
Result: Superhuman jumped from 22% to 33% through segmentation alone.

**Step 2 — Analyze feedback strategically**
- DO examine: "Very disappointed" users (what they love) + "Somewhat disappointed" who cite your main benefit (fixable gaps)
- DO ignore: "Not disappointed" users — lost causes, will request distracting features
- Use word clouds to surface patterns

**Step 3 — Build 50/50 roadmap**
- 50% doubling down on what users love
- 50% addressing blockers preventing "somewhat disappointed" from becoming enthusiasts
- Use cost-impact matrix (low/medium/high) to prioritize within each half

**Step 4 — Repeat obsessively**
Survey new cohorts continuously (never re-survey same users). Track "very disappointed" % as primary OKR.

**Results:** 22% → 33% (segmentation) → 58% (within 3 quarters).

## a16z Social App Benchmarks

Concrete thresholds for evaluating consumer social apps:

| Metric | OK | Good | Great |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monthly user growth | 20% | 35% | 50% |
| DAU/MAU | 25% | 40% | 50%+ |
| Day 1 retention | 50% | 60% | 70% |
| Day 7 retention | 35% | 40% | 50% |
| Day 30 retention | 20% | 25% | 30% |
| Week 1 retention | 40% | 55% | 75% |
| Week 4 retention | 20% | 30% | 50% |

- Paid marketing ≤10-20% of users at early stages
- Retention curve should flatten between d7-d14, plateau by d20
- If curve keeps declining → no PMF yet

## Leading Indicators of PMF (Pre-Scale)

Signs you're approaching PMF before you have scale metrics:
- Retention curve that **flattens** rather than declining to zero (Brian Balfour)
- Significant **organic growth**: word-of-mouth, direct traffic, branded search
- Users describe the product **in their own words** to others
- Users get **upset** when the product is down or a feature is removed
- NPS above 30 (strong for SaaS), above 50 (exceptional)
- Qualitative: users say they'd be "very disappointed" to lose it

## Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

### Core Concept
"What job is the customer hiring this product to do?" A "job" is the underlying progress a person wants to make in a particular circumstance — not a feature, not a demographic.

**Classic example:** People don't want a quarter-inch drill; they want a quarter-inch hole (or really: a hung shelf, or really: a comfortable home).

### Three Dimensions of Every Job
- **Functional:** The practical task (find people nearby, communicate)
- **Emotional:** How it makes them feel (less alone, confident, relieved)
- **Social:** How others perceive them (connected, respected, admired)

For product documents, always articulate all three dimensions. The emotional and social jobs are often more powerful motivators than the functional one.

### Job Story Format (preferred over user stories)
```
When [situation/trigger],
I want to [motivation],
so I can [desired outcome].
```

Better than user stories because it focuses on the triggering circumstance, not a demographic persona.

**Example for a social proximity app:**
"When I'm sitting alone in a coffee shop and notice someone interesting nearby, I want to know if we have something in common, so I can start a conversation with context instead of awkwardness."

### Two Schools

**Christensen (demand-side):** Focus on circumstances that cause someone to "hire" a product. Key technique: switch interviews — understand what caused someone to switch solutions.

**Ulwick (Outcome-Driven Innovation):** Structured 6-step process: define market as a job, identify all desired outcomes, quantify which are underserved, find opportunities, build solutions mapped to outcomes.

### Common Mistakes
- Feature overload without understanding core job
- Demographic assumptions instead of universal outcomes
- Ignoring context (time pressure, environment, emotional state)
- Measuring engagement instead of job completion
- Treating job statements as permanent
