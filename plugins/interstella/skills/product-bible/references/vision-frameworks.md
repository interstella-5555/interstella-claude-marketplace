## Marty Cagan (SVPG)

### 10 Principles for Product Vision
1. **Start with Why** — articulate purpose before solution
2. **Skate to where the puck is heading** — what's changing over 2-5 years?
3. **Fall in love with the problem, not the solution**
4. **Be inspiring** — a good vision inspires ordinary people to create extraordinary products
5. **Think big** — ambitious, possibly never fully reached
6. **Disrupt yourself** before competitors do
7. **Be stubborn on vision, flexible on details**
8. **Validate with customers early**
9. **Align with business and go-to-market strategy**
10. **Don't define a product roadmap** — vision is not a feature list

### Key Insights
- **Timeframe:** 2-5 years for software. One year = roadmap, not vision.
- **Format — the "Visiontype":** A prototype that dramatizes the future experience — short video, storyboard, interactive demo. Not a spec. Goes beyond scope of any single project.
- **Who creates it:** Head of product + 5-15 people (CEO, engineering, design, marketing) in a 1-2 day offsite.
- **Update cadence:** Rarely changes. Strategy shifts quarterly; vision does not.
- **Common mistakes:** Too narrow (one team), too short (1 year), abandoned prematurely, created without key leaders.

## Roman Pichler — Product Vision Board

Five sections on one canvas:

1. **Vision** — inspiring, shared, ethical, concise, ambitious, enduring (5-10 years)
2. **Target Group** — specific enough to tell who's in vs out
3. **Needs** — problems this group has that the product solves
4. **Product** — what it is and what makes it stand out
5. **Business Goals** — revenue, market entry, cost reduction, brand equity

Review at least every 3 months. Must be validated (not just hypotheses).

## Gibson Biddle — DHM Model

**How will your product delight customers, in hard-to-copy, margin-enhancing ways?**

### Delight
Understanding pain points, needs, desires. Creating emotional connection. Netflix: personalization went from 2% to 80% of choices via recommendations.

### Hard to Copy
Seven powers: brand, network effects, economies of scale, counter-positioning, switching costs, process power, cornered resources, unique technology.

### Margin
Sustainable business model — pricing experiments, cost reduction, operational efficiency.

### Exercise
1. List how your product delights today + future ideas
2. List hard-to-copy advantages (use seven powers as springboard)
3. List price/business model experiments for next 1-3 years
4. Select 4-6 hypotheses for testing over 12-24 months

## Lenny Rachitsky — Strategy Blocks

Product strategy sits between vision and execution. Three components:
1. A handful of **focus areas** (strategic pillars)
2. Areas explicitly **NOT in focus**
3. Clear **reasoning** for both choices

### Strategy Process (8-12 weeks)
1. **Preparation** (4 weeks): behavioral data, user research, leadership interviews, competitive analysis
2. **Strategy sprint** (1 week): share findings, identify problems, select 3-5 pillars
3. **Design sprint** (1 week): illustrative concepts
4. **Document writing** (1-2 weeks)
5. **Rollout** (2-3 weeks): stakeholder alignment

## Intercom — Product Principles

The most consistently useful part of any product doc — opinionated beliefs that guide daily trade-off decisions.

### What makes a good principle
- **Creates meaningful trade-offs** — if no one would disagree, it's not a principle
- **"We believe in quality"** = useless (who doesn't?)
- **"Opinionated by default, flexible under the hood"** = useful (the opposite — "maximally flexible by default" — is a legitimate choice)

### Intercom's structure
- 4 top-level R&D principles
- 5 product design principles
- 5 engineering principles
- Updated every 1-2 years

### Format for writing principles
```
[Belief statement]
Because: [reasoning — why this trade-off]
Even though: [the alternative someone might prefer]
Applied when: [specific situations where this kicks in]
```

## Competitive Positioning

### Positioning Table
Compare on dimensions that matter to YOUR user, not generic features:

| Dimension | Competitor A | Competitor B | Us |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core model | (their approach) | (their approach) | **Our differentiation** |
| Required attention | ... | ... | ... |
| Privacy | ... | ... | ... |

### Counter-Positioning
The strongest position is one where competitors can't copy you without undermining their own business model. Example: if competitors monetize through engagement/ads, you can position on "ambient, low screen time" — they can't follow without killing their revenue.
